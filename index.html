The Origin Story

This Publication Begins With a Father.
It Does Not End There.

Curtis Lee Wrenn Sr. had dementia. He was Penny Wrenn's father. He was brilliant, warm, specific — a person with a full life behind him and a future that deserved dignity. What he received, at the end, was what too many Americans with dementia receive: a system that was not designed for him, a healthcare infrastructure that treated his disease as a private family problem rather than a public health emergency, and a silence around his condition that bordered on shame.

This publication refuses that silence. It refuses the privatization of dementia — the quiet understanding that families manage this alone, that the system is doing what it can, that there is nothing structural to be done. There is everything structural to be done.

Dementia Deserves Better is a civic advocacy publication. It publishes the policy failures. It names the gaps in care. It connects the personal — the specific, irreplaceable human beings who have dementia — to the systemic, and it refuses to pretend that one does not require the other.

The Scale of the Failure

This Is Not a Rare Disease Problem.
This Is a Civic Infrastructure Crisis.

caregivers in the U.S.
$360B Annual cost of care —
mostly borne by families

disease-modifying cures

Those numbers describe a public health emergency. They are also numbers that most policy conversations treat as background noise — present, acknowledged, filed away. This publication does not file them away. It asks why the world's wealthiest nation has not solved this, and it names the forces that have prevented solutions.

Dementia is not a medical problem awaiting a medical solution. It is also a housing problem. A labor problem. A gender problem — because women provide the vast majority of unpaid care. A racial justice problem. An economic problem. A civic problem. It requires a civic response.

Building the Institutional Infrastructure
That Dementia Care Has Always Lacked

The CLW Foundation — named for Curtis Lee Wrenn Sr. — is the organizational home for this work. It exists because advocacy requires infrastructure, and because the personal grief that drives this publication should not be the only engine. Institutions outlast individuals. The Foundation is how this becomes structural.

The CLW Foundation advocates for policy change at the state and federal level. It supports caregivers — particularly unpaid family caregivers who are often doing the work of a healthcare system that has opted out. It publishes research, translates policy, and organizes communities around dementia as a civic issue, not a private one.

It is named for one man. It is built for millions.

Elder Care as Civic Infrastructure

The Framework: Dementia Policy
Is Infrastructure Policy

Caregiver Support

The 11 million unpaid caregivers providing dementia care are the invisible backbone of the healthcare system. Their labor deserves recognition, compensation, and relief.

Research Funding

The gap between dementia research funding and the scale of the crisis is a policy failure. Public investment in dementia research is not optional — it is overdue.

Dignity in Care

Every person with dementia is a full human being until the last day of their life. The systems that serve them must be built around that fact, not despite it.

Policy Accountability

Dementia Deserves Better tracks legislation, regulatory decisions, and funding allocations that affect the 7 million Americans with dementia and their families.

America 250 · Elder Care is Civic Infrastructure

A 250-Year-Old Republic
That Cannot Protect Its Elders
Has Work to Do.

In 2026, America turns 250. The measure of a civilization is how it treats its most vulnerable members. A republic that leaves 7 million people with dementia and 11 million caregivers without adequate support has not yet made good on its founding promises. That is what this publication covers.
